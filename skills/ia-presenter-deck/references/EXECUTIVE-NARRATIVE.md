# Executive Narrative Rules

Use this for:
- board updates
- quarterly reviews
- strategy memos turned into decks
- decision decks
- budget or prioritization asks

This file blends:
- Minto-style top-down logic and question-led structure
- Duarte-style audience movement and contrast

## Core Principle

Executive decks do not earn trust by sounding important.
They earn trust by making the logic easy to inspect.

## Opening Structure

Default opening:
1. `Situation`: what is true now
2. `Complication`: what changed, broke, or became risky
3. `Question`: what the room must now decide or understand
4. `Answer`: your top-line answer

This does not always need four separate slides.
Often it is:
- cover with governing thought
- short version up front
- one slide for what changed
- then the answer path

## Answer First

In executive contexts, say the answer early.

Do not make the room wait through:
- scene-setting that hides the conclusion
- a tour of all facts before the point
- suspense that only entertains the presenter

Good:
- We should narrow the rollout to one workflow.
- Q2 was healthier than it looked from bookings alone.
- The product is easier to adopt, but still too expensive to expand.

Bad:
- Background
- Context
- Update
- Key themes

## Build a Question Chain

Every section should answer a question raised by the section above.

Typical chain:
- What changed?
- Why does it matter?
- What are we doing about it?
- What proof do we have?
- What do we need next?

If a slide does not clearly answer one of those questions, it may be noise.

## Governing Thought

Each section needs a governing thought.

That means:
- a real claim
- short enough to hold in working memory
- strong enough to summarize the support below

Avoid blank summary lines such as:
- Three priorities
- Two key issues
- Five learnings

Prefer:
- The workflow improved before revenue did.
- Trust rose faster than expansion.
- The quarter got simpler after we cut scope.

## Deductive vs Inductive Use

Use deductive flow when:
- the recommendation is surprising
- the room will challenge the logic
- the argument needs to be defended step by step

Use inductive flow when:
- several observations point to one insight
- the room will accept the evidence if the synthesis is clean
- you want the support to converge on a clear conclusion

Do not mix them lazily on one slide.

## Grouping Rules

Support points belong together only when they share a real relationship.

Good grouping:
- three mutually distinct risks
- three steps in one process
- three causes of the same failure
- three proof points that justify one claim

Bad grouping:
- miscellaneous updates
- true facts that do not imply one insight
- points joined only by topic, not by logic

## Order Matters

Once grouped, order the points on purpose:
- time order for steps
- structural order for parts of a system
- strongest-first for arguments
- causal order when one thing drives another

If you cannot explain the ordering choice, the grouping is probably weak.

## Executive Slide Types

Especially useful:
- short version up front
- stop / start or before / after contrast
- full-width proof table
- risk slide
- ask slide
- one-line governing-thought reset

Less useful by default:
- decorative image-led openers
- quote slides with no decision value
- long workshop-style sequences

## What To Hide

Do not show everything.
But in executive decks, do not hide the actual answer either.

Hide:
- supporting explanation
- transitions
- implementation nuance
- caveats that matter more in discussion than on the slide

Show:
- the decision
- the claim
- the proof shape
- the risk shape
- the ask

## Final Test

The deck is working if:
- the answer is clear within the first few slides
- each section answers a visible or implied question
- each summary line earns the support below it
- the room can challenge the logic because the logic is visible
